FINAL PROJECT

GUIA WEAR - Smart Wristband for People with Visual Impairment

Final Project

📘 Project Overview

This project focused on the development of a smart wristband designed to support people with visual impairment during daily mobility. The wristband uses an ultrasonic sensor to detect nearby obstacles and activates a vibration motor whenever an object is detected within a predefined distance.

The project integrates product design, electronics, PCB manufacturing, programming, 3D printing, and IoT monitoring through the Blynk platform, resulting in a functional assistive technology prototype.

🧰 Materials and Components

PCB Manufacturing

Copper board, PCB design, soldering iron, tin solder, drill bits, cutting tools and cleaning materials.

Electronic Components

HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor, vibration motor, XIAO ESP32-C3, resistors, connectors, jumper wires, rechargeable battery and breadboard.

3D Printing Materials

TPU filament, nylon, printed wristband case, PCB holder, battery compartment and Prusa 3D printers.

Software & Tools

Arduino IDE, KiCad, USB-C cable, multimeter, soldering station and Blynk IoT platform.

📌 Phase 1 - Research of User Needs (Jacqueline Mayancela)

The first phase focused on identifying the needs of people with visual impairment. A QFD matrix was developed to relate user requirements with technical characteristics such as sensor range, vibration intensity, comfort, weight, battery life and ergonomic design.

The QFD analysis established the priorities that guided the entire development process.

✏️ Phase 2 - Initial Sketches and Concept Development (Jacqueline Mayancela / Mateo Ledesma)

Several design concepts were produced to evaluate dimensions, ergonomics, component placement and ease of assembly.

The sketches made it possible to determine the best location for the ultrasonic sensor, PCB, vibration motor and battery before beginning the CAD design.

🔌 Phase 3 - Breadboard Testing (Mateo Ledesma)

Before manufacturing the PCB, the electronic circuit was assembled on a breadboard to verify sensor readings, vibration activation and power stability.

This stage allowed wiring mistakes and polarity issues to be corrected before producing the final circuit board.

💻 Phase 4 - Programming and Code Development (Mateo Ledesma)

The software was programmed using Arduino IDE. The ESP32 continuously measures the obstacle distance and activates the vibration motor whenever the detected object is within the predefined safety distance.

🖥️ Phase 5 - Wristband and PCB Design (Jacqueline Mayancela)

Once the electronics and programming were validated, the PCB and the wristband housing were designed together to ensure compatibility between the electronic system and the physical prototype.

The objective was to create a lightweight, ergonomic and compact structure capable of securely holding the PCB, battery, sensor and vibration motor.

⚡ Phase 6 – PCB Manufacturing (Jacqueline Mayancela)

The PCB manufacturing phase included the preparation and production of the printed circuit board. The circuit design was printed and transferred onto the copper board so that the electronic traces could be created accurately.

After printing, the board was drilled to create the necessary holes for the electronic components. This process required precision because every hole had to match the exact position of each component before soldering.

This phase transformed the temporary breadboard prototype into a compact, organized and permanent electronic circuit suitable for integration into the wearable device.

🔩 Phase 7 – Circuit Assembly and Soldering (Mateo Ledesma / Tabata Vega)

Once the PCB had been manufactured, all electronic components were placed and soldered according to the circuit design.

The ultrasonic sensor connections, vibration motor, resistors, microcontroller pins and power connections were assembled carefully to guarantee proper operation.

During this stage, continuity tests were performed to verify that no soldering defects, short circuits or loose connections were present before integrating the PCB into the wristband.

Compared with the breadboard prototype, this version provided a much more stable, reliable and compact electronic system.

🖨️ Phase 8 – Wristband Printing and Physical Prototype (Karen León)

The wristband enclosure was manufactured using 3D printing technology to create a customized structure capable of holding every electronic component securely.

The prototype was designed to provide comfort, stability and an ergonomic fit while maintaining enough internal space for the PCB, battery, vibration motor and ultrasonic sensor.

After printing, dimensional inspections were carried out to verify component placement and overall fitting. Minor adjustments were considered to improve comfort and guarantee proper obstacle detection.

📊 Phase 9 – IoT Monitoring with Blynk (Mateo Ledesma / Jacqueline Mayancela)

To improve the capabilities of the smart wristband, an Internet of Things (IoT) monitoring system was implemented using the Blynk platform.

The ESP32-C3 microcontroller transmitted obstacle-distance measurements through Wi-Fi, allowing real-time monitoring of the wristband from a mobile dashboard.

The collected information generated graphs showing obstacle proximity throughout the day, providing valuable information for testing, performance evaluation and future product improvements.

✅ Phase 10 – Final Assembly and Testing (Mateo Ledesma / Tabata Vega)

During the final stage, every subsystem was integrated into a single functional prototype.

The PCB was installed inside the wristband enclosure together with the ultrasonic sensor, vibration motor and power supply.

Several functional tests were performed to verify obstacle detection accuracy, vibration response distance and the correct operation of the IoT monitoring system through the Blynk application.

The prototype successfully fulfilled the project's main objective by detecting nearby obstacles and notifying the user through vibration feedback.

🏆 Final Prototype

The final outcome of this project is a fully functional smart wristband capable of detecting nearby obstacles using an ultrasonic sensor and notifying users through vibration feedback.

The prototype successfully combines product design, embedded programming, PCB manufacturing, additive manufacturing through 3D printing and IoT monitoring using the Blynk platform.

Beyond obstacle detection, the device also records distance measurements, allowing digital visualization of user interaction with surrounding objects and providing valuable information for future improvements.

📌 Conclusions

Accessibility

The smart wristband provides a practical technological solution that increases safety and independence for people with visual impairment.

Engineering Integration

The project successfully integrates electronics, embedded programming, PCB manufacturing, CAD design, 3D printing and IoT technologies into a single functional device.

Product Development

Following a structured development methodology made it possible to transform user requirements into a reliable functional prototype.

Future Improvements

Future versions may include GPS positioning, rechargeable battery management, machine learning algorithms and mobile notifications to further enhance user experience.
